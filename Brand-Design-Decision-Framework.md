# Brand & Visual Design — Decision Framework

*A reusable playbook for making — and defending — brand visual-identity decisions. Written using **Polaris** as the worked example, but the method applies to any client or project.*

---

## How to use this document

This is a thinking tool, not a style guide. When you sit with a client and need to choose colours, type, or a visual direction, walk these steps in order. The goal is that **every decision can be defended in one sentence that traces back to a brand value.** "I like it" is not a reason; "this muted gold signals understated wealth, which matches their premium positioning" is.

The golden order, every time:

> **Strategy → Emotion → System → Execution**

Decide *who the brand is* and *what it should make people feel* before you touch a single colour or font. Most bad design is good execution of a decision that was never made.

---

## Part 1 — The mindset

1. **Decisions over preferences.** Taste is the starting point; a defensible rationale is the deliverable. Clients pay for judgement, not opinions.
2. **Constraints create brands.** A brand is defined as much by what it *refuses* as what it uses. One accent colour, two fonts, one idea. Restraint reads as confidence.
3. **Everything encodes meaning.** Colour, weight, spacing, contrast — the audience reads all of it subconsciously. Your job is to make those signals *deliberate* instead of accidental.
4. **The brief chooses the design.** If the strategy is clear (premium, independent, AI-era, trustworthy), the visual choices become logical deductions, not guesses.

---

## Part 2 — The colour decision framework (8 steps)

**Step 1 — Start from emotion, not colour.** Decide what the brand should make people *feel*, then find colours that produce that feeling. Each colour must earn its place by encoding a value.
*Polaris:* the brief was premium, trustworthy, independent, AI-era, memorable → that brief produced obsidian + gold + white.

**Step 2 — Assign roles before hues (the 60-30-10 rule).** Every palette has a *dominant* colour (~60%), a *secondary* (~30%), and an *accent* (~10%). Decide the *jobs* first — "a canvas, a content colour, one spotlight" — then fill them. This ratio is the difference between calm/expensive and chaotic.
*Polaris* runs an even stricter **70 / 25 / 5** (obsidian / white-grey / gold), because extra restraint signals extra confidence.

**Step 3 — Think in HSL, not hex.** Hex codes are just addresses. Designers reason in **Hue** (which colour), **Saturation** (how pure/intense), **Lightness** (how light/dark). Two rules to memorise: *lowering saturation makes anything feel more sophisticated* (a pure bright gold looks cheap; a desaturated champagne gold looks premium); and *you control contrast through lightness, not hue.*

**Step 4 — Build tonal ramps, not single colours.** A real brand never has "one black." It has a ramp — for Polaris: Ink → Obsidian → Surface → Hairline → Mist → Slate → Snow → White — because a product needs a background, a card on it, a border between them, and three tiers of text. The accent gets variants too (base / hover-light / pressed-deep). Hand a developer the full ramp, or they'll invent the in-between shades and the brand drifts.

**Step 5 — Use temperature and saturation to set mood and direct the eye.** Warm colours (gold, red) *advance*; cool colours (blue-black) *recede*. A warm gold on a cool near-black creates tension that makes the gold leap forward — which is why one gold button commands attention. Low overall saturation = calm, premium, grown-up; high saturation = energetic, playful, easily cheap.

**Step 6 — Enforce contrast and accessibility (non-negotiable).** The standard is **WCAG**: body text needs a contrast ratio of at least **4.5:1** against its background; **3:1** for large text (roughly 24px+, or 19px+ bold). This is legibility, and legibility *is* brand quality. Tool to memorise: the **WebAIM Contrast Checker** — paste two hex codes, read the ratio. Always test the *real* text colour on the *real* background, not swatch-on-white.

**Step 7 — Treat restraint as strategy.** The instinct is to add colours; resist it. One accent on a neutral base is the most reliable signal of a premium, confident brand — it says "we know exactly who we are." Most luxury brands live in black, white, and one colour. Not a coincidence.

**Step 8 — Test in context, never in swatches.** Colours lie in isolation. A gold that's gorgeous as a square can look sickly as text or garish as a full button. Always mock the palette up *applied* — a real headline, a real button, a real card — before you lock it.

## Part 3 — Colour emotion quick-reference

Associations are largely cultural and contextual (mostly Western conventions) — use as a starting prompt, not a law. Saturation and lightness shift the meaning as much as hue.

| Colour | Common associations | Watch-outs |
|---|---|---|
| Black | Sophistication, authority, luxury, mystery, premium | Can feel cold or funereal if unrelieved |
| White | Clarity, honesty, space, simplicity, calm | Can feel sterile or empty if overused |
| Grey | Neutrality, balance, professionalism, restraint | Can feel boring/non-committal |
| Gold | Value, quality, achievement, warmth, prestige | Bright/saturated = cheap or "casino"; mute it |
| Blue | Trust, stability, competence, calm (the default tech colour) | Over-used in tech; can feel cold or generic |
| Green | Growth, health, nature, money, "go" | Hue matters: lime = cheap, forest = premium |
| Red | Energy, urgency, passion, appetite, danger | Aggressive; hard to use as a base |
| Purple | Creativity, luxury, wisdom, the unconventional | Can feel mystical/niche |
| Orange | Friendly, energetic, affordable, playful | Rarely reads as premium |

## Part 4 — The typography decision framework

**Core idea: type is tone of voice made visible.** The same sentence in two fonts says two different things. Choosing type is choosing *how the brand sounds.*

**The emotional axes** (move along these to find the right voice):

- **Serif vs Sans-serif.** Serifs (little feet on the letters) read as established, traditional, editorial, trustworthy-with-heritage. Sans-serifs read as modern, clean, neutral, tech-forward.
- **Within sans, three families:**
  - *Geometric* (built from circles/straight lines — e.g. Poppins, Futura): friendly, modern, precise, a little cool.
  - *Humanist* (warmer, calligraphic roots — e.g. Inter is near-humanist): approachable, readable, human.
  - *Grotesque / neo-grotesque* (e.g. Helvetica; Space Grotesk is a quirky geometric-grotesque): confident, neutral-to-technical, contemporary.
- **Weight** (thin → black): light weights feel elegant, premium, airy; heavy weights feel loud, urgent, bold.
- **Width** (condensed → extended): condensed = urban, efficient, edgy; extended = luxurious, calm, spacious.
- **Contrast** (thin/thick stroke variation): high-contrast faces feel elegant/fashion; low-contrast feels sturdy/utilitarian.

**The pairing logic — "character on top, legibility underneath."** Pick a *display* face with personality for headlines and a *highly legible* face for body. They should **contrast** (so the hierarchy is obvious) but **harmonise** (shared proportions or era). A safe, professional default is *one distinctive sans for headlines + one neutral sans for body.*
*Polaris:* **Space Grotesk** (headlines) gives memorable, technical, AI-era character; **Inter** (body) gives effortless legibility and recedes politely. Character on top, clarity underneath — which mirrors the brand: a sharp guide delivering plain, honest substance.

**Hierarchy & spacing basics:**

- **Type scale:** pick a small set of sizes with a consistent ratio (e.g. 1.25× or 1.333× between steps) rather than arbitrary sizes.
- **Leading (line spacing):** body text wants ~1.4–1.7× the font size for comfortable reading; headlines want tighter (~1.1–1.2×).
- **Tracking (letter spacing):** loosen slightly for small uppercase labels; never track lowercase body text.
- **Measure (line length):** ~45–75 characters per line for readable body copy.
- **Two weights are usually enough** — one for body, one for emphasis. Restraint again.

## Part 5 — The vocabulary (glossary)

So you can speak the language with clients and developers.

**Colour**

- **HSL / HSB** — Hue, Saturation, Lightness/Brightness: the human-readable way to describe colour.
- **Hue** — the colour itself (red, blue, gold). **Saturation** — its intensity/purity. **Lightness/Value** — how light or dark it is.
- **Tint / Shade / Tone** — a colour plus white (tint), plus black (shade), plus grey (tone).
- **Tonal ramp / colour scale** — an ordered set of a colour's lightness steps (e.g. 50 → 900).
- **Contrast ratio** — measured difference in luminance between two colours; the basis of accessibility.
- **WCAG / AA / AAA** — Web Content Accessibility Guidelines; AA (4.5:1 body) is the standard target, AAA (7:1) is stricter.
- **60-30-10** — the dominant / secondary / accent proportion rule.
- **Gamut** — the range of colours a device or model can display.

**Typography**

- **Serif / Sans-serif** — with / without the small finishing strokes ("feet").
- **Geometric / Humanist / Grotesque** — the three main sans-serif sub-families (see Part 4).
- **x-height** — the height of a lowercase "x"; larger x-heights read better at small sizes.
- **Weight** — stroke thickness (thin, light, regular, medium, bold, black).
- **Kerning** — spacing between specific letter *pairs*. **Tracking** — uniform spacing across a run of text. **Leading** — vertical space between lines.
- **Type scale** — the chosen set of sizes and the ratio between them.
- **Measure** — the length of a line of text.
- **Ligature** — two characters joined into one glyph (e.g. "fi").

**Brand strategy**

- **Positioning** — the space you own in the customer's mind vs competitors.
- **Archetype** — a personality pattern (Sage, Hero, Outlaw…) that makes a brand feel human and consistent.
- **Avatar / persona** — a specific portrait of the target customer.
- **Value proposition** — the concise promise of the value you deliver.
- **Hero / Guide** — the StoryBrand framing: the *customer* is the hero, the *brand* is the guide who hands them a plan.

## Part 6 — Learning resources (foundational / pre-AI)

These are established, human-authored texts and tools — the canon professional designers actually learn from. *Titles and authors are accurate to the best of my knowledge; confirm current editions and links before purchase, as URLs and editions change.*

**Brand strategy (fast, high-leverage reads)**

- **Marty Neumeier — *The Brand Gap*** and ***Zag***. Short, visual, and the quickest way to "get" brand strategy. Start here.
- **Alina Wheeler — *Designing Brand Identity***. The comprehensive, process-oriented reference for running a brand project end to end.
- **Donald Miller — *Building a StoryBrand***. The hero/guide messaging framework (the one used in the Polaris brand story).

**Colour**

- **Josef Albers — *Interaction of Color***. The foundational text on how colours behave *in relation to each other* (the "colours lie in isolation" principle).
- **Johannes Itten — *The Elements of Color*** (the condensed Itten). Classic colour theory — the colour wheel, contrasts, temperature.
- **Sean Adams — *The Designer's Dictionary of Color***. Practical, modern, colour-by-colour associations and usage.

**Typography**

- **Ellen Lupton — *Thinking with Type***. The friendliest practical intro to type, hierarchy, and pairing. Best starting point.
- **Robert Bringhurst — *The Elements of Typographic Style***. The deep "bible" of typography for when you want mastery.

**Layout, UI & accessibility**

- **Josef Müller-Brockmann — *Grid Systems in Graphic Design***. The classic on grids and structured layout.
- **Adam Wathan & Steve Schoger — *Refactoring UI***. Extremely practical, modern guidance on colour, spacing, hierarchy, and UI polish.
- **Steve Krug — *Don't Make Me Think***. The classic on usability and clarity.

**Tools (free, web-based)**

- **WebAIM Contrast Checker** (webaim.org) — paste two colours, get the WCAG ratio.
- **Adobe Color** (color.adobe.com) — build and test palettes, extract colours from images.
- **Coolors** (coolors.co) — fast palette generation and exploration.
- **Google Fonts** (fonts.google.com) — free, licensable type; good for testing pairings.

*Tip: read* The Brand Gap *and* Thinking with Type *first — together they're a weekend, and they cover ~80% of what you'll use day to day.*

## Part 7 — One-page decision checklist

Before you lock any visual decision, answer:

1. **What feeling** should this create, and **which brand value** does it encode?
2. Have I assigned **roles** (dominant / secondary / accent) before picking hues?
3. Am I reasoning in **HSL** — is saturation low enough to feel premium (if that's the goal)?
4. Have I built a full **ramp**, not single colours, so the system survives real use?
5. Does **temperature/saturation** point the eye where I want it?
6. Does every text/background pair pass **WCAG AA (4.5:1)** — checked, not guessed?
7. Am I being **restrained** — one accent, two fonts, one idea?
8. Have I tested it **in context** — real headline, real button, real card?

If you can answer all eight, the decision is defensible. That's professionalism.
